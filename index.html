HUMAN-WILDLIFE
INTERACTIONS RESEARCH

A mobile-based app for researchers to digitally collect Human-wildlife research data from hikers
who are enjoying the informative functions of the application in return for participating in the study.

The final solution fruitfully achieves the research aim, supplying an important contribution to human-wildlife academic research, the easy to use interface allows it to be adapted for further study outside Scotland.

Introduction

The purpose of this research project is to investigate the effects of hikers on wildlife and their displacement in the North Chest-Hill Estate in Northern Scotland. Our team was working with Dr Long and his researcher group in Sustainable development department, University of St Andrews.

The original data-collection method of this research was taken using hand-written survey cards, however this had issues as hand-written survey cards are time-consuming and impacted by weather. Digitisation of physical records requires a high cost, the recorded data is often inaccurate, invalid and not consistent in structure and format, additionally it is not possibility to gather photo or video data. Our task was to develop a fast, easy, simple and instantaneous data-collection user-centre interaction solution for this research.

Prototype

The final stage of the process was to use the designs to create a final prototype solution with basic citizen science functions and extra beneficial functions for hikers. The solution developed by our team meets all of Dr Long’s requirements and satisfies the most important expectation: fast, easy, simple and instantaneous.

Basic citizen science functions

The citizen science functions have three main components (1) First-time set up process (2) Requiring ‘Hiking sessions’ (3) The interaction recording process.

Extra beneficial functions

In the basic citizen science functions, hikers would likely only use the app because they were recruited to participate and contribute to a citizen science project. Yet these extra beneficial functions offer the hikers some personal benefits in exchange for their participation and contribution to the citizen science.

(1) Planning trips and downloading maps

(2) Review previous hiking statistic and recordings

Conclusion

Both solution work well to efficiently ameliorate issues experience by Dr Long’s study’s manual data collection and data entry processes. The creative solution goes on step further and aim to help recruit participants in the study as well. Now both solutions have been implemented and under analysis process. This app would be used for all hikers across Scotland and the UK as whole, not merely just for research being conducted within the North Chest-Hill Estate. It could drastically alter how human-wildlife interactions are studied and explored in the future.
